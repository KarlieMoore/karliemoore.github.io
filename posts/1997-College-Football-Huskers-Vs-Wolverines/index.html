There’s nothing quite like a good ol’ fashion college football rivalry to get fans in an uproar! Whether it’s Army vs Navy, Michigan vs Ohio State, or Oklahoma vs Texas, college football will always draw the biggest debates amongst fans about which team is the best. Perhaps the greatest dispute in college football history happened in 1997 when the Nebraska Huskers, with a record of 13-0, and the Michigan Wolverines with a 12-0 season, split the National Championship the year before the start of the Bowl Championship Series. Here is the 26-year-old question: Who was actually the better team? They never met each other on the field but were both deemed champs. Ultimately, the National title was given to the Nebraska Huskers who won the Coaches’ Poll. Most Michigan fans will claim that was only because Tom Osborne retired and had nothing to do with who was actually better. If the two teams had played in 1997, how would it unfold? Who would win what has been named “The Game of the Century That Never Was?”

While both the 1997 Nebraska and Michigan teams didn’t play each other, they did play a couple of the same teams during the course of their season. By examining the results of these games, it may give some indication of whether the Huskers were the true 1997 champs. Both Nebraska and Michigan played Colorado. As seen by the data in the waffle chart, it is clear that Michigan defeated Colorado by a greater margin. Nebraska won 27-24 over Colorado, while the Wolverines defeated them by a score of 27-3, a much larger margin of a win. Are there other things to consider in when comparing these games? Definitely. For example, did Colorado have more injuries and bigger players out during the Michigan game than during the Nebraska game? Does when they played their games have an impact? Michigan convincingly defeated Colorado on September 13, 1997, while the Huskers win came on November 28, 1997. Did Colorado progress and get better and stronger as the season progressed giving Nebraska a better challenge? While there are things that may play a role, it is clear that Michigan’s win over Colorado is too much greater to ignore. Perhaps it proves a stronger defense than the Huskers.

During the 1997 season, Nebraska and Michigan also both played Baylor. Nebraska defeated the Baylor Bears 49-21 on October 11. Michigan took a win from Baylor by a score of 38-3 on September 20. Again, a bigger win by the Wolverines than the Huskers. However, some claim this game irrelevant since other factors, again, come into play. Michigan played Baylor at home and Nebraska played them on the road, and the standard 3.5 point home field advantage swing wipes out Michigan’s 7 point difference, rendering those Baylor performances the same. So…I guess we are not much further ahead at answering who was actually the better team.

The only two similar games Michigan and Nebraska played in 1997 were Colorado and Baylor, which really weren’t the strongest teams in either division. Perhaps comparing the strength and difficulty of each team’s overall season will help determine who was really the best team in the nation. When looking at comparing all of the teams in both the Big 10, Michigan’s Conference, and the Big 12, Nebraska’s Conference, Michigan had a tougher go, having the 6th most difficult season schedule, while the Huskers had the 10th ranked level of difficulty. Nebraska played four teams ranked in the top 25 while Michigan would play seven teams ranked in the top 25. When comparing the teams played, Michigan played 6 teams in the regular season that earned Bowl berths. Nebraska only played four teams to qualify for Bowl Games. However, of those six teams that faced the Wolverines, they all went 0-6 in their Bowl matches, while the four teams the Huskers played went 2-2 in their Bowl Games. Another reason the teams were “co-champions” in 1997. Even their seasons schedules and outcomes appear to be equal.

Are final scores the only way to determine the best team, or is how they play the game an indication of success? In football, how the ball is advanced up the field can give an indication of true skill. By examining Nebraska and Michigan’s passing and rushing game for the 1997 season, a final determination of the true National Champion might arise. Is controlling the rushing or passing game better for victory? In the football world, there is the great debate about whether passing or rushing is the measure of true skill. It is believed by some football enthusiasts that the rushing game is positively correlated with the probability of winning. Some say many teams will run or rush the ball to take time off the clock, but there is the argument that throwing the ball on offense gains more yards. During the 1997 season, the Huskers played a much stronger rushing game than the Wolverines, while Michigan had close to equal rushing and passing yards. While at first glance it may look like the Huskers should be the better team, you have to consider that they may have spent more time rushing the ball because their scores were closer,and they were trying to just end the games safely with a win. Michigan may have more passing yards because they were winning by larger margins and didn’t feel the need to run the ball as much. So, it’s a draw!

Well, the team schedules of the 1997 season don’t give us a definitive answer as to who were actually National College Football Champions. The scores in head-tohead games against similar teams doesn’t make it any clearer, and because of conflicting ideas about whether passing or rushing makes for a better team, we seem no further ahead in determining the best college football team of 1997. Maybe we can go to the experts. The Associated Press poll (AP poll) provides weekly rankings of the top 25 NCAA teams in Division I college sports football. The rankings are compiled by polling 62 sportswriters and broadcasters from across the nation. These are experts in the field who know college football inside-out, and who make their living deciding who is the best. As seen in the line graph, the Huskers took an early lead in the first four weeks, fell in the stats in week 5, came back on top for the next six weeks before the Wolverines were best in the National rankings for the final four weeks. What does this mean? Nebraska was first for 10 of the 15 weeks. Does this mean they essentially should be deemed the National Champions because of the poll. It looks pretty clear, but there are some faults with relying on these stats. Consider biases. Who knows if the sportswriters and broadcasters went to the schools they are ranking or have been a long-time fan? Another issue: College football provides only 12-13 games each season to evaluate a team. With this small sample size, teams can look much better by their record than they deserve. While the Huskers look to be the chosen season for the 1997 season, relying on opinions may not be the most accurate way to decide the real champs.

I think this evidence proves one thing. Until two teams play each other, there truly is no 100 percent way to know who was better. The fans of the Big House will always believe it is Michigan, and the fans that pack Memorial Stadium weekends in the Fall will stand by their Huskers as the true National Champions of the 1997 season. And while I have my own opinion (Go Big Red!), I fear that the question of who were actually the National Champs will stay a great college football mystery.
